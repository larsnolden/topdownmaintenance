## Before You Start / Safety

- Park on flat ground; handbrake on.
- Final tightening must be with a **calibrated torque wrench**.
- Do not use impact gun torque as your final value.

## Required Tools

- 1/2" torque wrench
- 21 mm socket (common OEM NB lug-nut size)
- Breaker bar (loosening only)
- Wire brush/rag for hub face and nut seats
- Optional paint marker for completion marks

## NB wheel hardware baseline

- Stud thread: **M12 × 1.5**
- OEM-style wheel seat: generally **60° conical/taper**
- Practical stock-road target: **108 N·m (80 ft·lbf)**

If you use aftermarket nuts/wheels, confirm seat style and manufacturer torque value first.

### Seat-style compatibility check (critical)

Do not mix seat types:
- conical nut on ball-seat wheel,
- ball-seat nut on conical wheel,
- mag/shank hardware on tapered seat.

Seat mismatch can loosen under load even when torque was “correct” at installation.

## Step-by-Step Procedure

### 1) Inspect before tightening

Check for:
- damaged/stretched studs,
- cracked or deformed nut seats,
- rust scale between wheel and hub,
- oily/dirty threads (clean and dry threads are preferred unless hardware maker says otherwise).

### 2) Hand-start every nut

Start each nut by hand for several turns to avoid cross-threading.

### 3) Snug in star sequence

Bring nuts down gradually in a criss-cross/star pattern so wheel seats evenly on hub face.

For 4-lug NB wheels, use a diagonal cross pattern (1-3-2-4 style) and repeat until all nuts are evenly snug.

### 4) Final torque in two passes

With wheel prevented from rotating:
1. First pass at ~70-80 N·m
2. Final pass at **108 N·m (80 ft·lbf)**

Then do one final confirmation pass in the same star order.

Best practice:
- set torque wrench to zero/lowest scale after use,
- pull smoothly at handle center (no jerky pulls),
- do not “double-click” repeatedly on the same nut.

### 5) Re-torque after short service interval

Re-check lug torque after **50-100 km (30-60 mi)**, especially after:
- recent wheel removal,
- new wheels/nuts/studs,
- track/autocross heat cycles.

### 6) If one nut repeatedly loosens

Stop driving at speed until resolved. Inspect for seat mismatch, stretched stud, incorrect nut profile, or wheel-seat damage.

## Verification / Post-service checks

- No steering shimmy/vibration at road speed
- No clicking from wheel area on low-speed turns
- All nuts rechecked at interval and still at target torque

## Common failure flags after wheel service

- One nut turns significantly before reaching target on re-torque
- Metallic clicking after cornering load changes
- Conical seat witness marks look uneven or gouged

If any appear, inspect stud/nut/wheel seat condition before further high-speed driving.
